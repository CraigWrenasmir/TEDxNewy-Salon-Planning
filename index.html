TEDxNewy Salon: Newcastle 2050 – What If?

Room design, facilitator guides, and asset checklists

Overview Transport Health Night-Time Runsheet Data Facilitators Cards Assets

All rooms invite participants to imagine Newcastle in 2050. Through a mix of digital input, hands-on design, and open discussion, participants will build ideas together.

Three interaction modes run across every room:

Reflect (Screen Input)

Digital prompts and live audience responses

Build (Table Activity)

Collaborative design using maps, paper and tokens

Debate (Wall Prompt)

Provocative discussion prompts to spark conversation

How will we move through, in and out of Newcastle in 2050?

Design Principles

With ~70 participants across 3 rooms and 3 rotations (~23 per room per session), and a small team managing changeovers, data capture must be built into the activities themselves — not bolted on as an extra task.

  • The activity IS the data. Participants create the data artefact by doing the task. Stickers on overlays, filled-in cards, screen responses — these are the outputs. No one needs to separately transcribe or record.
  • Clear overlay sheets on all maps. Each session gets a fresh acetate/transparency sheet over the base map. Participants place stickers on the overlay, not the map. Between sessions, label the sheet (Room + Session #), set it aside, lay a fresh one down. At the end of the night, stack all three overlays to see where consensus clusters. No photos needed between sessions.
  • Structured response cards over sticky notes. Free-form sticky notes from 9 table-sessions are hard to analyse. Pre-printed cards with scaffolded fields give you consistent, comparable data across all groups.
  • Screen prompts = free digital data. Anything collected via Mentimeter/Slido is automatically captured, timestamped, and exportable. Use 2 prompts per room: one quantitative (poll/ranking) and one qualitative (open text). This is your cleanest data source.
  • One end-of-night photo circuit. After the final session, one person photographs: overlay stacks (layered and individual), wall prompts, and any remaining table artefacts. Everything else is already collected in labelled envelopes.

Transport Room — Data Collection

Transport Priority Ranking Quantitative Digital

Screen poll: "Rank your top 3 transport priorities for Newcastle 2050" — forced choice from a set list (e.g. light rail expansion, cycling network, airport link, pedestrian CBD, autonomous transit, ferry services, regional rail, accessibility upgrades). Gives you a clean bar chart of what matters most.

Capture: Auto-captured via Mentimeter/Slido. Run once per session (3 datasets).

Open Screen Prompt Qualitative Digital

"What would make Newcastle a world-class transport city?" — open text responses on screen.

Capture: Auto-captured. Exportable word cloud + raw responses.

Infrastructure Heatmaps Spatial

Both maps (CBD + Greater Newcastle) get a clear overlay per session. Participants place coloured dot stickers to mark where infrastructure should go. Use a simple colour code: one colour for "priority location", another for "connection point".

3D tokens (train, light rail, bike, etc.) are used as discussion tools during the activity. When a group agrees on a placement, they mark it with a sticker on the overlay — the sticker is the data, the token is the conversation starter.

Capture: 2 maps × 3 sessions = 6 labelled overlay sheets. Stack at end of night for composite heatmap. Photograph stacked and individual.

Route Mapping Spatial Qualitative

Pins and string on the base map for desired routes and connections. Since these can't go on an overlay, photograph each table's route layout at the end of each session (3 photos per map = 6 total). Facilitator can quickly snap these during the 5-minute changeover.

Capture: Quick photo at end of each session before string is reset. Label photos.

Health & Wellbeing Room — Data Collection

Investment Token Allocation Quantitative

Each table gets a set number of tokens and a printed allocation board with categories (green space, active transport, community hubs, youth mental health, quiet/rest spaces, wellbeing services). Groups distribute tokens across categories. A simple tally sheet at each table records the count.

With 2 tables × 3 sessions = 6 tally sheets, you get a clear picture of where people want investment, and can compare across sessions.

Capture: Facilitator or table volunteer counts tokens and marks the pre-printed tally sheet before sweeping tokens back for the next group. ~30 seconds per table.

Where Novocastrians Feel Well Quantitative Digital

Screen poll: "Where in Newcastle do you feel well?" — multiple choice from a list of precincts/areas (Foreshore, Merewether, Blackbutt, Darby St, Honeysuckle, Civic Park, etc.) plus an "Other" free-text option. Gives you a countable precinct ranking AND qualitative colour from the "Other" responses.

Capture: Auto-captured via Mentimeter/Slido. Run once per session.

Capture: Auto-captured. Can be presented as a word cloud in real-time.

End-of-Night Data Collection Protocol

Session 3 wraps at 8:10 pm. One person per room does a quick circuit while volunteers guide guests downstairs for closing remarks at 8:15 pm. Total time: ~5 minutes per room.

  1. Overlay sheets: Already labelled and set aside after each session. Stack all overlays on the base map and photograph from above (composite view). Then photograph each overlay individually. (~3 photos per map)
  2. Wall prompts: Photograph the full sticky note wall as-is. (~1 photo per room)
  3. Remaining table artefacts: Photo any pin-and-string routes (Transport), token boards (Health), or Glow Map details (Night-Time) still on tables.
  4. Collect envelopes: Gather all labelled card envelopes (should already be sorted by session). One bag per room.
  5. Tally sheets: Collect the token allocation tally sheets from Health & Wellbeing.
  6. Screen data: No action needed — already saved digitally. Export after the event.

Materials to Prepare

  • Clear overlay sheets / acetate: At least 15 sheets (6 for Transport — 2 maps × 3 sessions, 6 for Health — 2 maps × 3 sessions, 3 for Night-Time Glow Map only — persona table uses response cards instead of overlays, plus spares). Cut to map size. Source from office supply or art stores — OHP transparencies or clear acetate rolls work.
  • Dot stickers: 4 colours minimum. Green, red/orange, yellow/amber, and blue. Standard office dot stickers (8–10mm). ~200 per colour should be ample.
  • Pre-printed cards: Transport Change Cards (~75), Healthy Day Cards (~20), Persona Night Cards (~20), Memory Cards (~40). Print on card stock, A5 size.
  • Tally sheets: 6 copies of the investment allocation tally (Health & Wellbeing room).
  • Labelled envelopes: 9 large envelopes (3 rooms × 3 sessions), pre-labelled with room name and session number.
  • Marker pens: Fine-tip permanent markers that write on acetate (e.g. Sharpie ultra-fine). For participants who want to annotate overlays.

General Approach

  • Briefly set the scene with local context, trends, or tensions
  • Circulate and prompt deeper thinking across tables
  • Offer light reality-checks without shutting ideas down
  • Help identify patterns and themes during debriefs
  • Connect ideas back to the broader future of Newcastle
  • Tone: welcoming, creative, informed, and optimistic — while inviting engagement with real tensions and trade-offs

Room 1: Transport & Mobility

Focus: Encourage thinking about both everyday usability and big-picture infrastructure. Prompt discussion around accessibility, reduced car dependence, regional links, tourism, and how transport shapes the experience of city life.

  • What transport changes would most improve daily life for residents?
  • How could the CBD connect to the wider region, airport, and future growth areas?
  • What would make Newcastle feel innovative, accessible, and well-connected by 2050?
  • Challenge: By 2050, what should Newcastle feel like to move through, and what must we design differently now?

Room 2: Health & Wellbeing

Focus: Help participants think beyond healthcare toward the daily environments that shape wellbeing. Consider who the city currently supports, where friction exists, and what investments could make Newcastle healthier and more inclusive.

  • Health is shaped by daily environment as much as healthcare systems
  • Access to nature improves mental health and longevity
  • Walkable cities reduce chronic disease
  • Social connection is a major predictor of wellbeing
  • Urban isolation and sedentary lifestyles are growing risks
  • Challenge: By 2050, what should Newcastle feel like as a place to live well, and what must we design differently now?

Room 3: Night-Time Culture & Economy

Focus: Encourage broad thinking about night culture — not only bars and restaurants, but live music, arts, festivals, transport, safety, accessibility, late-night activity, and who the city is designed for after dark. Knowledge of Newcastle's nightlife history is beneficial.

  • Newcastle's nighttime culture has a rich and complex history
  • The city is actively trying to rebuild and diversify its night economy
  • Night-time culture includes live music, theatre, food, festivals, public life, transport, and creative experiences
  • A great night city must work for different people, not just one type of patron
  • Challenge: By 2050, what should Newcastle feel like after dark, and what must change to make that possible?
